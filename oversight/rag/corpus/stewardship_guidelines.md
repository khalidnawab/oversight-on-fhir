# Antimicrobial Stewardship Guidance (synthetic summary for demo; grounded in published guidelines)

## Antibiotic time-out and review timing
A formal reassessment of antimicrobial therapy — an "antibiotic time-out" — should occur at
48–72 hours, when culture and susceptibility data are typically available (CDC Core Elements of
Hospital Antibiotic Stewardship Programs). The Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021 recommends daily
assessment for de-escalation rather than a single checkpoint. De-escalation is usually actionable
on day 3–4 once susceptibilities have resulted; multi-hospital evidence shows day-4 de-escalation
is as safe as continued broad-spectrum therapy while reducing antibiotic days and length of stay.

## De-escalation on culture results
When culture and susceptibility results are available, narrow empiric broad-spectrum therapy to the
narrowest effective agent that covers the identified organism, absent contraindications (IDSA/SHEA
2016 Implementation Guideline; Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021). For methicillin-susceptible
Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA), cefazolin or an antistaphylococcal penicillin is preferred over
vancomycin. For susceptible Enterobacterales such as E. coli, a narrower beta-lactam (e.g.
ceftriaxone) is appropriate. Document the rationale for the de-escalation decision.

## Culture-negative infection and stopping therapy
Negative or unobtained cultures do NOT justify indefinite broad-spectrum therapy. When cultures are
negative and the pretest probability of bacterial infection is low, reassess whether infection is
present and consider STOPPING antibiotics (Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021; CDC Core Elements). If a
syndrome is still likely, de-escalate empirically based on the clinical picture and the local
antibiogram rather than isolates. Roughly a third to half of sepsis is culture-negative; absence of
data is a reason to reassess, not to continue broad coverage indefinitely.

## Duration of therapy
Shorter, evidence-based courses are preferred and are one of the highest-yield stewardship levers.
Representative durations for uncomplicated infection with adequate source control: community-acquired
pneumonia ~5 days; uncomplicated Gram-negative bacteremia ~7 days; complicated intra-abdominal
infection ~4 days after source control; pyelonephritis 5–7 days. Duration is largely syndrome-based
and does not require culture data.

## Intravenous to oral (IV-to-PO) switch
Switch from IV to oral therapy once the patient is clinically improving and meets criteria:
hemodynamically stable and not on vasopressors, afebrile trend (commonly ~24 hours), able to tolerate
oral intake with a functioning GI tract, and an oral agent with adequate bioavailability is available
(IDSA/SHEA 2016). Early IV-to-PO switch reduces line complications and length of stay without
compromising outcomes for many infections.

## Procalcitonin
Procalcitonin, interpreted alongside clinical judgment, can support decisions to stop or shorten
antibiotics — particularly in lower respiratory tract infection and sepsis. It should be used as an
adjunct to clinical assessment, not as a sole trigger to start therapy (Surviving Sepsis Campaign 2021).

## Prospective audit and feedback
Prospective audit and feedback (PAF) — reviewing active antimicrobial orders and giving clinicians
concrete recommendations — is one of the two core, evidence-based stewardship interventions alongside
preauthorization (CDC Core Elements; IDSA/SHEA 2016). Automated identification of patients due for a
de-escalation review is a computable form of PAF.
